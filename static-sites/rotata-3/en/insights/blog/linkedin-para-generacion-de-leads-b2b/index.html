Do you need to generate more leads? Trust LinkedIn for B2B lead generation.

LinkedIn is a conversational network, it seeks debate.

If you think about LinkedIn for B2B lead generation you are well on your way, LinkedIn is no longer just a showcase for sharing professional achievements. It's much more: it has become a conversational sales channel, especially valuable for B2B businesses that are looking for new opportunities without resorting to traditional methods such as cold calling (increasingly less useful) or mass email marketing. Although the temptation to use LinkedIn as a simple commercial megaphone is strong, true success lies in understanding that, at its core, this platform is, above all, a space for genuine conversation, where human connections precede any commercial intention.

Seek to help, not give talks.

No one connects to LinkedIn expecting to receive a sales pitch. Just like at a dinner between professionals, the first contact should seek to start a sincere conversation, not force a sale. It is essential to change the approach: instead of “closing meetings” from the first message, the initial objective should be to provoke a response, open an authentic dialogue and find points of common interest. It's about building relationships that, over time, can become real and sustainable business opportunities.

The importance of your profile.

To take advantage of the full potential of LinkedIn, I recommend that you start with yourself, with your own profile. You have to stop thinking of it as a mere online resume and transform it into a landing page that speaks for and for your potential clients, not about your professional achievements. An effective profile must answer three questions clearly and convincingly:

  • Who are you?
  • What do you do for your clients?
  • Why should they trust you?

Regarding the owner, instead of limiting yourself to describing the position you occupy, I recommend that you reflect in a concrete and attractive way the value you provide. A message like “I help B2B companies scale their demand generation with AI” has a much greater impact than a generic “Marketing Director.” Of course, forget the "Account Manager", they are an invitation not to contact you.

The profile image also plays a crucial role: conveying professionalism and closeness can be as simple as a natural smile captured by a professional photographer. I recommend that you invest in good photography; It is, in reality, investing in your personal brand. Forget selfies and photos in a football stadium.

Generate content and participate.

However, optimizing the profile is not enough. You have to be present: commenting, publishing and sharing relevant content is part of an active strategy that enhances visibility and credibility. On LinkedIn, comments have even more impact than your own posts when it comes to increasing organic exposure. Each thoughtful comment is an additional touchpoint, an opportunity to showcase expertise and generate genuine interest.

The content you share on LinkedIn should be carefully designed to generate value. Real customer stories that illustrate their before and after, trend analysis, short videos that share unique insights, and comparisons based on concrete data are the formats that truly capture attention. Each post should be an invitation to conversation, not a promotional monologue.

Contact yourself directly, do not delegate it.

When the time comes to prospect, I recommend that you get involved directly. Do not completely delegate this task to assistants, a big mistake that I have personally made in the past, since the first conversations shape the perception that the prospect will have of your organization. You can use playbook automation tools, like The Growth Machine, but you have to get in the driver's seat early in the conversation to be successful.

When it comes to automation, it is imperative that you find the right balance. Automating repetitive tasks, such as shipping scheduling or data collection, can free up valuable time, but it should never eliminate genuine personalization. Ideally, at least 20% of each message should be specifically tailored to the recipient's context and needs. That attention to detail is what makes the difference between being ignored and being remembered.

I also recommend that you explore new communication formats. Have you thought about sending videos or voice messages?

We are all very used to receiving text messages, but we receive much fewer videos and voice messages, which you can record with the LinkedIn app on your mobile. If you want to be different, it's time to go for it. If you can write a text of interest, I'm sure you can also record it in a voice message or even a video. Bring out your reporter side and dare to do something different. Once again, test and measure the results; Remember that there are no magic formulas and many times controlled "trial-error", the famous A/B tests, must be a relevant part of your B2B strategy.

Choose your audience carefully.

Prospecting does not mean shooting messages indiscriminately. It is a task of surgical precision: searching for profiles that fit perfectly with a well-defined ICP (Ideal Client Profile), filtering by industry, position, company size and, fundamentally, by their recent activity on LinkedIn. In LinkedIn Navigator you have special filters to know which users are active on LinkedIn and which are not. Trying to contact inactive profiles not only consumes resources, but also undermines team morale.

I have material, an excellent profile and a list of potential clients, now what?

Now it's time to connect, and the way to connect also requires strategy and subtlety. There are various schools of thought: some experts advocate sending connection requests without a message, trusting that a well-optimized profile speaks for itself; others prefer to accompany the request with a short personalized message. For example: «Hello Elena, I have seen your work in the IT sector. At Rotata we help companies like yours improve lead conversion with AI without expanding the team. Shall we connect? The key is to be brief, direct and relevant.

As always, you can do A/B tests to see what works best in your specific case. Personally, I prefer to either leave it blank and let my profile speak for me, or use a short message directed at a specific problem and with a challenging whole, always without falling into an aggressive message.

Once the connection is established, the rhythm of interaction should be natural and respectful. During the first few days, I recommend that you send a sincere thank you message, accompanied by some useful content to open the dialogue. If there is no response, a few days later you can share an additional resource or relevant reflection. Aggressive insistence is usually counterproductive; Sometimes, it's just not the right time, and the wisest thing to do is to keep the door open for future opportunities.

Here is a suggested sequence:

  • Day 1-2: Be grateful for the connection and share a useful piece of information.
  • Day 3-5: If this does not get you an answer, provide more valuable content, without losing focus of the topic you are raising.
  • Day 6-8: If there is still no response, make one last attempt by offering a valuable conversation or some content of interest.
  • Day 10-12: If we finally fail to start a valuable conversation, make an elegant closing: share interesting content or say goodbye professionally.

We are already using LinkedIn for B2B lead generation. How do we now measure the success of our campaigns?

Forget vanity metrics like likes or followers. Measuring results is essential, but knowing what to measure is even more so. Beyond "likes" and followers, the truly relevant indicators in a B2B strategy are:

  • Response rate to your messages,
  • Accepted connection ratio,
  • Qualified leads generated,
  • Scheduled meetings.

Analyzing this data will allow you to refine the strategy, identify points of improvement and maximize the return on each effort invested.

One last piece of advice.

And, finally, a principle that you should never forget: on LinkedIn, consistency and authenticity are worth more than any attempt at fleeting virality. It's not about publishing content that explodes one day and disappears the next. It is about building, day after day, a credible, solid and relevant presence. Publishing at least twice a week, interacting daily with the network and maintaining a flexible but consistent editorial calendar can make a radical difference in the medium and long term.

With strategy, patience, authenticity and a human touch, LinkedIn can stop being just another social network and become the soul and engine of sustainable growth for any B2B business that aims to dominate its sector.
