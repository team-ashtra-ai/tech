The B2B guide so that your emails arrive, are read and generate a pipeline

Imagine that today you are asked to lead an email campaign in which your company has high expectations: it is about opening conversations with high-value accounts and positioning your company as a reliable channel to generate pipeline now and in the future, and you do not want your B2B emails to end up in spam. The quickest way to ruin it, and the one that usually starts, is with the email issue. The right way is to start where almost no one else does: the authentication layer, domain health, and reputation. I tell you the complete path, in order, with the why and risks of each decision.

The invisible foundation: SPF, DKIM, DMARC and BIMI

Before going into detail with these four concepts, keep in mind that we are talking about technical standards for sender authentication and identity. If technology is not your thing and this sounds very technical, don't worry, your role is not to configure it, but to validate with your IT team that it is done. Here's a high-level explanation of each of these elements:

  • SPF is the list of who can send emails on behalf of your domain. This way the recipients (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo...) know that, for example, HubSpot or SendGrid are authorized. If it's not right, your emails may look fake and end up in spam.
  • DKIM guarantees that the message has not been manipulated along the way, which protects both your reputation and the security of the recipient. Without DKIM, anyone could spoof your emails, and providers might start to distrust anything that comes from your domain.
  • DMARC ties all this together: it makes sure that the domain visible to the user is actually yours and establishes what to do if something fails (allow, quarantine or reject the message). If DMARC is not configured, questionable messages can go unchecked, increasing the risk of spoofing or your emails ending up in spam folders.
  • BIMI, for its part, adds a visual layer of trust: display your logo in the inbox, reinforcing brand recognition and perceived security by the recipient. It is especially useful in brand campaigns or newsletters, where visual recognition drives openness and trust.

It is very important to highlight that these technical aspects are not an added value, they are essential requirements for it to make sense to start sending emails. If SPF/DKIM/DMARC don't align, don't continue, you'll only end up with B2B emails ending up in spam, it's better to delay a day than lose domain reputation for weeks. Talk to your IT team and make sure everything is working correctly.

Prepare the domain and protect your reputation

Before sending your first campaign, make sure the domain or subdomain you will send from is ready. Using a specific subdomain for campaigns has a clear advantage: it separates the reputation of commercial email from the rest of corporate communication, preventing a problem in your sending from affecting the entire organization. For example, you could use mail.yourcompany.com or news.yourcompany.com for your campaigns, while your usual email remains info@yourcompany.com; This way, if something goes wrong with your marketing mailings, your corporate email is not affected. If you just set it up, start small. Sending hundreds of emails from a newly created domain is like suddenly showing up at a party and starting shouting: no one will trust you. Start with active contacts, small, consistent sends, and gradually increase. This way, the filters learn that you are a legitimate sender.

For example, if you launch a new newsletter for your company, you could start by sending it only to the internal team and a few trusted clients. When you see that everything is working well—the emails arrive, are opened, and are read—you gradually expand to larger lists. That way, the server learns to trust your domain and you gain confidence that everything is working correctly before escalating.

Database cleanliness and quality

Your database is the fuel for email marketing, but if it is dirty, the engine stops. Before each shipment, check that your contacts are current, that the addresses are not duplicated or misspelled, and that no one has been added without your consent. Think that sending someone who is not expecting you is like showing up without an invitation: they may open the door for you, but no one guarantees that your visit will be welcome.

For example, imagine that you run a campaign to present a new feature of your product. If your list includes old or inactive contacts, many won't remember who you are and will mark your email as spam. Instead, if you filter by those who interacted in the last three months or downloaded a recent resource, your open and response rates will skyrocket. A “spam report” hurts more than an “unsubscribe”, because in addition to losing the contact, it affects the reputation of your domain and can mean that the following emails do not even reach the inbox.

A single objective per campaign

When preparing your next campaign, choose a single clear objective: either get a meeting, or get a response, or encourage a download. Trying three things at once—meeting, demo, and download—often confuses the reader and reduces effectiveness. Focus the entire message on that action: from the subject and the previous text that accompanies the subject to the body of the email and the call to action (CTA). This way each part of the message pushes in the same direction and avoids losing attention to distractions.

For example, if the goal is to get a conversation, the CTA could be “Can you fit in a 15-minute call this week to see if it makes sense?” If the goal is to spark interest, you can propose “I'll send you a one-page summary and tell me if it fits.” In both cases, the advice is to keep a single action per mail; the others can be addressed in the following messages.

Write for people (and filters)

The text of your emails should sound human, not advertising. This is where it's a good idea to have the HubSpot article “The Ultimate List of Email Spam Trigger Words” on hand (https://blog.hubspot.com/blog/tabid/6307/bid/30684/the-ultimate-list-of-email-spam-trigger-words.aspx). Avoid words and tones that filters associate with spam: exaggerated urgency, impossible promises or sales shouts. Instead of “HUGE DISCOUNT!” or “UNIQUE OFFER!”, explains what the reader gains and why it is worth their time. It is better to say “I'll share a resource that can save you work” than “Take advantage today and multiply your results!”.

The issue is not to impress, but to be credible. Think of it as the phrase you would use if you had three seconds to convince someone to open your email. The preview text, for its part, should complement the message, not repeat it. For example, if the subject is “How to reduce the time of your campaigns without spending more,” the preview text can add context: “I'll tell you the three automations that save the most time.” If you promise something in the subject line, make sure you deliver in the body: filters and readers detect inconsistency quickly.

Personalize with meaning

Personalization is not about putting names in brackets. It's about showing that you know who you're talking to. If you write to a marketing director, mention a challenge that you know her team faces or a relevant piece of information from her sector, such as difficulties integrating new tools or measuring the return of a campaign. For example, you can write something as simple as: "I've seen that your team is working with several automation platforms; we've just helped other companies in the sector simplify that process." But don't go overboard: using private or excessive information generates rejection. Good personalization is what provides context, not what invades.

Design a sequence that breathes

Not everything is decided with a single email. Effective campaigns combine several spaced messages and, above all, different channels. For example, the first email can present the proposal, the second delve into a benefit, and the third invite you to chat or schedule a meeting. Meanwhile, you can reinforce the message from other fronts: an interaction on LinkedIn to thank a post or comment on something relevant, followed by a brief call to close the conversation started by email.

Imagine that you send a first email with a useful resource, a few days later you connect on LinkedIn and thank them for their interest, and finally you make a call to offer to answer questions. That combination keeps the conversation alive without being pushy and creates a more natural relationship. In short, a good sequence breathes and flows between channels, like a conversation that is taken up at different times.

Evaluate what matters

Measuring matters as much as sending. Opens tell you who saw you, but responses and conversions tell you who really believed you. It measures everything that connects with business: delivery rate, opens, clicks, responses, conversions and scheduled meetings. Each metric tells you a different story. For example, a low delivery rate may indicate reputation problems or technical configuration errors; A high open rate with few responses suggests that the subject matter is engaging, but the content is unconvincing; a low click-through rate may reveal a lack of clarity in the call to action; and a low positive response rate will tell you that perhaps the message is not reaching the right audience.

To improve results, review your reports analytically: try different subject lines and preview texts, vary the sending time or day of the week, and experiment with different lengths and tones. Document what you learn in each campaign, because behavioral patterns repeat themselves and anticipating them gives you an advantage. Remember that data is not the end, but the map: it shows you where to go and what paths to avoid.

Ethics and perseverance: the true differentiator

Email marketing is not about finding the trick, but about building trust. Don't exaggerate results, don't force false emergencies, don't disguise promotions as exclusives. In the long term, contacts who trust you open, read and respond more. Remember that impeccable authentication, clean foundation, honest messages and human cadence are the combination that truly builds brand and pipeline. That's the difference between sending emails and building relationships.
